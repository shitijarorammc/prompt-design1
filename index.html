Prompt Designing
for Physicians

AI tools give better answers when you prompt them better. This guide covers three evidence-based prompting strategies — when to use each, why it works, and what it looks like in clinical practice.

Authors: Shitij Arora, MD · Sunit Jariwala, MD

CoT
Chain of Thought
Think step by step
For complex reasoning — make the model show its work
▶
Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting instructs the model to decompose a complex problem into a sequence of explicit intermediate reasoning steps before producing a final answer. Rather than jumping to a conclusion, the model is asked to reason through the problem out loud — mimicking the kind of deliberate thinking a physician does when working through a differential or treatment plan.
How it works
1
Frame the problem — give the model a specific clinical scenario with all relevant details
2
Instruct step-by-step reasoning — add a phrase like "think through this step by step" or "reason through each consideration before answering"
3
Read the reasoning, not just the answer — the reasoning chain is where errors surface; the final answer alone can look correct even when the logic is flawed
Clinical example
Prompt
A 68-year-old male with HFrEF (EF 25%), CKD stage 3 (eGFR 34), and hyperkalemia (K+ 5.4) presents for medication optimization. He is currently on carvedilol 25mg BID and furosemide 40mg daily. Think through step by step which additional evidence-based therapies should be considered, what contraindications or precautions apply for each, and in what order they should be introduced.
Use when
Differential diagnosisReasoning through competing diagnoses with overlapping presentations
Treatment sequencingDeciding the order of therapy additions in complex patients
Risk-benefit analysisWeighing procedure risk against clinical benefit
Multi-factor decisionsAny scenario with several interacting variables (renal function, drug interactions, comorbidities)
Not the right tool when
Avoid CoT for simple lookup questions ("what is the half-life of metoprolol") or when you just need a concise summary. Asking the model to reason through something that doesn't require reasoning produces verbose, unhelpful output.
Sahoo et al. (2024), arXiv:2402.07927 — see CoT section
CoVe
Chain of Verification
Make the model check its own work
For fact-sensitive outputs — structured self-audit to reduce hallucination
▶
Chain of Verification (CoVe) is a four-step prompting strategy in which the model generates an initial response, then independently plans and answers targeted verification questions about that response, and finally produces a revised answer incorporating what it found. The key insight is that verification questions are answered independently from the original response — preventing the model from simply echoing its prior output. Research shows CoVe reduces hallucinations while maintaining factual accuracy.
The four steps
1
Generate — the model produces a baseline response to your question
2
Plan verification — the model identifies specific factual claims in its response that could be wrong and frames them as questions
3
Answer independently — each verification question is answered on its own, without reference to the original response
4
Revise — the model updates its original answer based on the verification findings
Clinical example — two-turn approach
Turn 1 — initial response
What are the current guideline-recommended anticoagulation options for a patient with non-valvular atrial fibrillation and a CHA₂DS₂-VASc score of 4? List each agent, its typical dosing, and any key renal dose adjustments.
Turn 2 — verification prompt
Now identify any specific factual claims in your response that could be incorrect — such as dosing thresholds, eGFR cutoffs, or guideline class of recommendation. For each one, answer the verification question independently (without looking at what you said before), then revise your full response to correct any errors you find.
Use when
Drug dosingAny time specific doses, thresholds, or cutoffs are included in the output
Guideline specificsWhen you need class of recommendation or level of evidence to be accurate
List-based answersResponses listing multiple agents, criteria, or steps — each item is a potential error
High-stakes decisionsAny output you might act on clinically without independently verifying every fact
Not the right tool when
Avoid CoVe for creative tasks (drafting patient education, writing an abstract structure) where factual hallucination isn't the primary risk. It adds turns and length; use it selectively for outputs with consequential facts.
Sahoo et al. (2024), arXiv:2402.07927 — CoVe; original: Dhuliawala et al. (2023)
VS
Verbalized Sampling
Ask for options, not just one answer
For open-ended tasks — unlock diverse outputs by requesting a distribution
▶
Verbalized Sampling (VS) addresses a subtle problem: post-training alignment causes LLMs to converge on a single "typical" answer, suppressing the full range of plausible responses. VS works by asking the model to explicitly generate multiple distinct responses along with a probability or confidence weight for each — rather than producing one answer. This recovers diversity from the model's underlying training and gives you a richer set of options to evaluate. Research shows VS improves output diversity 1.6–2.1× without sacrificing quality or accuracy.
How it works
1
Reframe your prompt — instead of "give me an approach to X," ask for "5 distinct approaches to X with a relative probability or confidence for each"
2
Request the tails — optionally add "include less common or unconventional options, not just the most typical answer" to push the model away from its default mode
3
Select and apply your judgment — review the distribution of options and apply clinical expertise to choose the most appropriate for your patient
Clinical example
Prompt
Generate 5 distinct framings for how to explain the risks and benefits of ICD implantation to a 72-year-old patient with HFrEF who is reluctant and has low health literacy. For each framing, provide a brief description and a relative probability that it would resonate with this type of patient. Include both conventional and less typical communication approaches — not just the most standard explanation.
Use when
Patient communicationFinding the right frame for a difficult conversation — consent, behavior change, diagnosis delivery
Drafting & writingWhen one version of a patient handout, letter, or abstract isn't enough to evaluate
BrainstormingGenerating a differential, a research question, or QI project ideas where breadth matters
Educational contentCreating varied case scenarios, vignettes, or teaching points across a spectrum of difficulty
Not the right tool when
Avoid VS when you need one authoritative, fact-checked answer. If you're asking what the correct eGFR threshold is for dose-adjusting apixaban, multiple options with probabilities is not helpful — and potentially dangerous. Use CoVe instead.
Zhang et al. (2025) — Verbalized Sampling, arXiv:2510.01171
